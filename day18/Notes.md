
Day18 – Introduction
---------------------

Previous stages of the Collapse Lab investigation have progressively narrowed the observable characteristics 
of the residual latency spike phenomenon while eliminating several broad classes of potential explanations.

Day16 demonstrated that latency spikes persist under low-intrusion sampling conditions and are not 
accompanied by detectable anomalies in coarse-grained system-wide metrics. Despite observing spikes exceeding 200 ms, 
including events approaching 800 ms, no corresponding changes were identified in sampled CPU, interrupt, 
or softirq activity within the temporal vicinity of those events.

Day17 further refined the observation framework by increasing temporal resolution and aligning measurements 
precisely with phase transition boundaries. Under these conditions, 
spike behavior exhibited clear structural separation across phases. 
Extreme latency events were not observed during the intervention phase, 
but instead appeared at transition points, most notably at the onset of recovery R1. 
In addition, a distinct pattern of sub-threshold latency deviations was observed, 
primarily during recovery R2, 
indicating that recovery is not a uniform state but composed of multiple regimes with differing latency characteristics.

Together, these results establish two critical constraints. 
First, the spike phenomenon is not directly expressed during sustained load conditions. 
Second, spike occurrence is not uniformly distributed across execution phases, 
but instead shows alignment with transition boundaries within the cycle structure.

However, a central inconsistency remains unresolved. 
Despite the apparent association between spike events and phase transitions, 
the behavior is not deterministic. 
In Day17, a high-magnitude spike occurred only in one of three cycles and did not repeat under identical phase structure and timing. 
This suggests that phase alignment alone is insufficient to fully account for the observed behavior.

This raises a more focused theoretical question regarding the mechanism underlying spike emergence. 
If spike events are not driven solely by transition timing, 
then their occurrence may depend on accumulated system state that is intermittently released at specific transition points. 
Under this view, the transition boundary does not generate the spike directly, 
but instead acts as a trigger for the release of a latent condition that has developed during prior execution.

Conversely, if the spike mechanism is inherently transition-driven, 
then precise manipulation of transition timing should be sufficient to control spike occurrence, independent of accumulated state.

The distinction between these two possibilities - transition-driven behavior versus state-dependent release - 
defines the central problem addressed in this stage.

Hypothesis
-----------
The latency spike behavior observed in previous stages is consistently aligned with phase transition boundaries, 
yet remains non-deterministic across identical cycle structures. 
This suggests that transition alignment alone is insufficient to fully explain spike occurrence.

Two competing mechanisms are proposed:

Transition-Driven Mechanism
----------------------------
Under a transition-driven model, 
latency spikes are directly caused by the timing of phase transitions themselves. 
In this case, the transition boundary introduces a short-lived disturbance in execution timing, independent of prior system history.

If this mechanism is correct, then:

	- spike occurrence should be tightly coupled to the timing of phase transitions
	- modifying transition timing alone should shift the temporal location of spikes
	- spike probability should remain stable across cycles when transition structure is preserved
	- spike occurrence should not depend on accumulated activity from previous phases
	- spike occurrence should be reproducible across cycles under identical timing conditions
	
Under this model, the transition acts as the primary causal trigger of the latency event.

State-Dependent Mechanism
--------------------------
Under a state-dependent model, latency spikes are not generated by the transition itself, 
but are instead the result of accumulated system state that is intermittently released at transition boundaries.

In this case, the transition acts as a release point rather than a generator of the spike.

If this mechanism is correct, then:

	- spike occurrence should vary across cycles even under identical transition timing
	- altering the duration or structure of preceding phases should change spike probability
	- identical transitions may or may not produce spikes depending on prior system history
	- spike timing may remain near transitions, but its occurrence will not be deterministic.
	- spike occurrence should exhibit cycle-to-cycle variability even under identical timing conditions.
	
Discriminating Prediction
--------------------------

The two mechanisms can be distinguished experimentally by independently manipulating transition timing and preceding phase structure.
If spike behavior follows transition timing regardless of prior state, the transition-driven mechanism is supported.
If spike behavior varies with accumulated state despite identical transition timing, the state-dependent mechanism is supported.

Experimental strategy
----------------------
Day18 is structured as a comparison between multiple timing conditions designed to decouple:

	- the timing of phase transitions
	- the amount of activity accumulated before those transitions

To achieve this, Day18 introduces controlled variation in cycle timing while keeping the probe type and execution environment unchanged.

The experiment is divided into three timing conditions.

Condition A - Baseline timing condition
-----------------------------------------

This condition reproduces the Day17 phase structure and serves as the reference condition.

Cycle structure:

	- baseline = standard
	- intervention = standard
	- recovery R1 = standard
	- recovery R2 = standard
	- post-baseline = standard

Purpose:

preserve direct comparability with Day17
provide a reference spike distribution under the known timing pattern.

Condition B - Transition-shift condition
--------------------------------------------
This condition changes the timing of phase boundaries while minimizing changes to total activity generated during the cycle.

The main manipulation is:

	- shorten or lengthen selected recovery intervals
	- shift the timing of R1 and R2 boundaries relative to the intervention window
	- keep intervention behavior unchanged

Purpose:

test whether spike timing follows transition boundaries themselves
evaluate whether latency events relocate when phase transitions are moved in time

Interpretation:

	- if spike timing shifts consistently with the altered boundary timing, this supports the transition-driven model.
	- if spike timing does not track the moved boundaries, this weakens the transition-driven model.
	
Condition C - State-load condition
------------------------------------
This condition preserves the transition structure as closely as possible while modifying the amount of prior activity available to accumulate before recovery.

The main manipulation is:

	- change the duration of intervention and/or preceding steady-state phases
	- preserve the qualitative order of phases and boundary sequence
	- keep heartbeat measurement and transition labeling unchanged

Purpose:

test whether the probability or magnitude of spikes depends on the amount of prior phase activity rather than the transition boundary itself.

Interpretation:

	* if spike occurrence changes with the amount of preceding activity while transition timing remains comparable, this supports the state-dependent model.
	* if spike behavior remains unchanged despite altered prior activity, this weakens the state-dependent model.

Analysis plan
--------------
After the run completes, analysis will compare across conditions:

	- number of spikes exceeding 200 ms
	- maximum observed latency
	- distribution of spike events across phases
	- temporal distance between spike events and nearby transition boundaries
	- distribution of sub-threshold high-latency deviations in the upper tail

Expected discriminators
------------------------
The transition-driven model is favored if:

	- spike events move in time with adjusted phase boundaries.
	- similar boundaries produce similar latency behavior across cycles.
	- spike behavior is relatively insensitive to changes in preceding phase duration.

The state-dependent model is favored if:

	- spike probability or magnitude changes with preceding phase duration.
	- spike behavior differs across cycles despite preserved transition timing.
	- altered activity structure changes spike occurrence without requiring major boundary relocation.
	
Expected limitations
--------------------
Several limitations are acknowledged:

	- complete separation of transition timing and accumulated state may not be perfect within the current cycle framework.
	- rare extreme events may still require multiple cycles before stable patterns emerge.
	- heartbeat timing can reveal event structure and phase alignment but does not directly identify kernel execution paths.
	- absence of deterministic behavior under a given condition does not necessarily eliminate either model, 
	  but may indicate that additional latent variables remain uncontrolled.
	 

For this reason, Day18 is treated as a model-discrimination step rather than a final attribution step.

Results
--------
Three experimental conditions were executed, each consisting of three full cycles. 
Each cycle included BASELINE, INTERVENTION, RECOVERY_R1, RECOVERY_R2, and POSTBASELINE phases. 
Heartbeat sampling was maintained at 50 Hz across all runs.

General Observations

Across all conditions:

	- Heartbeat intervals during BASELINE and POSTBASELINE remained stable, with values consistently in the ~21–22 ms range.
	- No sustained degradation of heartbeat timing was observed outside RECOVERY_R2.
	- All fio interventions produced consistent throughput of approximately 8207 KiB/s, with ~64 MiB written per run segment.
	- Retry activity during RECOVERY_R2 produced intermittent latency values (dt_ms) ranging from ~50 ms to several hundred milliseconds.
	
Condition A
	* Total spikes (dt_ms > 120 ms): 23
	* Spikes appeared shortly after the start of RECOVERY_R2 in all three cycles.
	* In each cycle, spikes were concentrated in the early portion of RECOVERY_R2.
	* The majority of non-spike values remained in the ~50–60 ms range.
	* Maximum observed spike: ~296 ms (from earlier logs), with most spikes between ~130–200 ms.
	* Spike density decreased over time within RECOVERY_R2.
	
Condition B
	* Total spikes (dt_ms > 120 ms): 36
	* Spikes appeared shortly after the start of RECOVERY_R2 in all three cycles.
	* Spike clusters were denser compared to Condition A.
	* Spikes extended further into RECOVERY_R2, occupying a larger portion of the phase.
	* Multiple high-magnitude spikes were observed, including values exceeding 300 ms.
	* Non-spike values continued to cluster around ~50–70 ms.
	* The transition into RECOVERY_R2 consistently preceded the onset of elevated latency values.
	
Condition C
	* Total spikes (dt_ms > 120 ms): 20
	* Spikes appeared shortly after the start of RECOVERY_R2 in all three cycles.
	* Spike distribution was similar in structure to Condition A.
	* Spike density was lower than Condition B and comparable to Condition A.
	* Several high-magnitude spikes were observed, including values above 200 ms and a peak near ~291 ms.
	* Non-spike values remained stable around ~50–60 ms.
	* Spike activity was concentrated in the early portion of RECOVERY_R2 and decreased over time.

Temporal Alignment

Across all conditions:

	- The onset of elevated dt_ms values consistently occurred shortly after RECOVERY_R2_START markers.
	- No clustering of spikes was observed during BASELINE, INTERVENTION, or RECOVERY_R1 phases.
	- Spike timing aligned with phase transitions rather than with absolute timestamps within the cycle.

Conclusions
---------------
The results demonstrate that latency anomalies are initiated by phase transitions rather than by steady-state load.
Across all conditions, latency spikes (dt_ms > 120 ms) appeared exclusively after the transition into RECOVERY_R2. 
No comparable spike patterns were observed during BASELINE, INTERVENTION, or RECOVERY_R1. 
Instability is therefore not continuously present under load, but emerges at a specific phase boundary.

Condition B provides direct causal evidence. 
Advancing the onset of RECOVERY_R2 by shortening RECOVERY_R1 resulted in a corresponding shift of the spike cluster, 
along with increased density and extended duration. 
The relocation of instability in time, precisely aligned with the modified transition boundary, 
establishes the transition itself as the triggering event, independent of absolute time or accumulated workload.

In contrast, Condition C increased the duration of the INTERVENTION phase without altering the transition structure. 
Despite the higher accumulated workload, the spike pattern remained comparable to Condition A in both magnitude and temporal distribution. 
Increased load alone does not amplify or sustain the instability.

Taken together, these results reject a steady-state or load-driven degradation model. 
The system exhibits phase-sensitive behavior in which instability is triggered upon crossing a specific boundary.

The severity of the instability is modulated by the system state prior to the transition. 
Incomplete recovery, as introduced in Condition B, produces higher spike density and a longer instability window. 
The transition acts as the trigger, while the pre-transition state determines the intensity and persistence of the response.

The instability is transient. 
Following its onset in RECOVERY_R2, latency returns toward baseline within the same phase, 
indicating entry into a temporary unstable regime followed by self-stabilization.

High-magnitude spikes, including outliers exceeding 250–300 ms across multiple conditions, 
indicate involvement of deeper system-level mechanisms in latency amplification. 
These events are confined to the instability window and are not uniformly distributed, 
suggesting interaction with underlying components beyond the retry mechanism.

Overall, Day18 establishes a boundary-driven operational mode in which latency instability is triggered by phase transitions and shaped by recovery state, 
rather than by continuous load accumulation.

The underlying mechanism remains unresolved. 
The current results do not distinguish whether the instability originates from scheduler dynamics, block-layer behavior, 
or interactions between retry logic and kernel-level resource management. 
Day19 will introduce targeted instrumentation and controlled separation of these components to isolate the source of the transient instability regime.
